The Aurora River Project is a long-term environmental research program focused on studying freshwater ecosystems in the northern valleys of the Greenstone region. The project began in 2019 after local scientists noticed that seasonal flooding was changing the behavior of fish, insects, and riverbank plants. Since then, the research team has monitored water quality, species diversity, and soil movement along a 42-kilometer stretch of river.

The main research station is located near Pine Bridge, a small village known for its wooden footpath and old stone dam. From this station, scientists travel to three observation zones named North Bend, Willow Point, and Marsh Corner. Each zone has different conditions. North Bend is fast-moving and rocky, Willow Point is shaded by tall trees, and Marsh Corner has slow water and dense reeds. These differences make the river ideal for comparison studies.

One of the most important findings of the project is that the silver minnow, a small native fish, is more sensitive to temperature changes than previously believed. During the summer of 2021, the team recorded a sharp decline in silver minnow activity when water temperatures rose above 24 degrees Celsius. In contrast, the reed frog population increased during the same period because the warmer water supported more insects.

The project also works closely with local schools. Students from Pine Bridge Secondary School visit the station every spring to learn about sampling methods and habitat protection. The researchers use these visits to teach the importance of careful observation, data recording, and environmental responsibility.

A final goal of the Aurora River Project is to create a public conservation guide for the region. The guide will recommend ways to reduce river pollution, protect nesting areas, and preserve native species. The team expects to finish the first version of the guide in 2027.
